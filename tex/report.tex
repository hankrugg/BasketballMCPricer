\section{Project scope}

This project prices and tracks one basketball prediction-market contract: a
Kalshi ``Yes'' contract on Oklahoma City defeating Phoenix in the April 22,
2026 playoff game. If Team A is Oklahoma City and Team B is Phoenix, the payoff is
\begin{equation}
    X = \mathbf{1}\{S_A(T) - S_B(T) > 0\},
\end{equation}
where \(S_A(t)\) and \(S_B(t)\) are cumulative team scores and \(T=48\)
minutes is the regulation horizon. Since the contract settles within a single
game, the model price is interpreted directly as a win probability:
\begin{equation}
    V_t = \mathbb{P}\left(S_A(T) - S_B(T) > 0 \mid \mathcal{F}_t\right).
\end{equation}

The notebook keeps the scoring parameters fixed from pregame calibration and
asks how a simple Monte Carlo model reprices the contract as the observed score
and time remaining change throughout the game.

\section{Scoring model}

Each team's score is modeled as a compound Poisson scoring process. Scoring
events arrive with team intensity \(\lambda_i\) per minute, and each event has
mark \(Y \in \{1,2,3\}\), representing a free throw, two-point basket, or
three-point basket. The pooled mark distribution is estimated from the 2024--25
NBA regular season:
\[
    p_1 = 0.2884, \qquad p_2 = 0.4805, \qquad p_3 = 0.2311.
\]
Thus the expected points per scoring event are
\[
    \bar y = p_1 + 2p_2 + 3p_3 = 1.9426.
\]

Let \(N_{i,k}(t)\) be the number of \(k\)-point scoring events by team \(i\)
through time \(t\). Under the model,
\[
    N_{i,k}(t) \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_i p_k t),
\]
and the team score is
\begin{equation}
    S_i(t) = N_{i,1}(t) + 2N_{i,2}(t) + 3N_{i,3}(t).
\end{equation}
The model assumes independent increments and independent team scoring processes.
Its useful computational feature is that a winner contract depends only on the
terminal margin.

\section{Monte Carlo pricing process}

The Monte Carlo pricer samples terminal scoring counts rather than every
scoring time. For a pregame price, each simulation \(j=1,\dots,M\) draws
\[
    N_{A,k}^{(j)}(T) \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_A p_k T),
    \qquad
    N_{B,k}^{(j)}(T) \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda_B p_k T),
\]
for \(k=1,2,3\). It then forms the simulated final margin
\begin{equation}
    P^{(j)}(T)
    =
    \sum_{k=1}^3 kN_{A,k}^{(j)}(T)
    -
    \sum_{k=1}^3 kN_{B,k}^{(j)}(T),
\end{equation}
and the simulated payoff
\begin{equation}
    X^{(j)} = \mathbf{1}\{P^{(j)}(T) > 0\}.
\end{equation}
The model price and standard error are
\begin{equation}
    \widehat V_M = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{j=1}^M X^{(j)},
    \qquad
    \widehat{\mathrm{SE}} =
    \sqrt{\frac{\widehat V_M(1-\widehat V_M)}{M}},
\end{equation}
with a clipped approximate 95\% confidence interval
\(\widehat V_M \pm 1.96\,\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}\).

The live version uses the same logic from an observed in-game state. If the
current margin is \(m_t=S_A(t)-S_B(t)\) and the remaining regulation time is
\(\tau=T-t\), the simulation samples only future scoring over \(\tau\). The
live price is then estimated as
\begin{equation}
    \widehat V_t
    =
    \frac{1}{M}\sum_{j=1}^M
    \mathbf{1}\{m_t + \Delta P_\tau^{(j)} > 0\}.
\end{equation}
This is the role of \texttt{price\_from\_state}: it adds the known margin to
simulated future margins and returns the probability that Oklahoma City wins.

\section{Game calibration}

The remaining unknowns are \(\lambda_A\) and \(\lambda_B\). The notebook uses
two sources of market information. First, the ESPN pregame sportsbook line
listed a total of 216.5 points and Oklahoma City as a 17.5-point
favorite.\footnote{ESPN game page:
\url{https://www.espn.com/nba/game/_/gameId/401869368}.} This gives a
spread-plus-total calibration:
\[
    \mathbb{E}[S_A(T)+S_B(T)] = 216.5,
    \qquad
    \mathbb{E}[S_A(T)-S_B(T)] = 17.5.
\]
Since \(\mathbb{E}[S_i(T)] = \lambda_i T\bar y\), these two equations split
the total scoring intensity between the teams.

Second, the notebook uses the Kalshi pregame OKC ``Yes'' price and the same
216.5 total. The total fixes
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_A + \lambda_B
    =
    \frac{216.5}{48\bar y}
    =
    2.3218.
\end{equation}
The calibration searches for \(\lambda_A\), sets
\(\lambda_B=2.3218-\lambda_A\), and runs the Monte Carlo pricer until the
model win probability matches the Kalshi pregame probability of 0.9137. The
code uses a coarse grid to find a bracket and Brent's method to solve for the
final value.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
Calibration & \(\lambda_A\) & \(\lambda_B\) \\
\midrule
Spread plus total & 1.2547 & 1.0671 \\
Kalshi yes plus total & 1.3246 & 0.9972 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The live path uses the Kalshi-yes-plus-total fit, so the model agrees with the
observed pregame winner market before the game starts. Later differences are
driven by realized score, time remaining, Monte Carlo error, and information in
the market that the simple scoring model does not represent.

\section{Live game path}

The notebook loads two time series: minute-level Kalshi prices and a
reconstructed NBA scoring-event path. The raw timestamps are stored in UTC, so
the notebook converts them to Eastern time before analysis. For example,
2026-04-23T01:30Z is April 22 at 9:30 PM ET, the local game date.

Scoring events are irregular, while Kalshi prices are minute bars. To align the
data without leaking future information, the notebook carries forward only the
latest known score state to each Kalshi timestamp. It records the OKC margin,
elapsed minutes, remaining time, and contemporaneous Kalshi price, then runs
\texttt{price\_from\_state}. The live model path is therefore a sequence of
independent Monte Carlo repricings, one per Kalshi timestamp.

For the OKC--PHX game, Oklahoma City won 120--107. The first in-game Kalshi
price was 0.9198, while the model path started at 0.9203 after applying the
Kalshi-yes-plus-total calibration. Across the game window, the mean absolute
difference between the model fair value and the Kalshi price was 0.0098, with
an RMSE of 0.0127 and a maximum absolute gap of 0.0404. Near the end of the
game both the market and model moved close to one as Oklahoma City's lead
became difficult to overcome.
